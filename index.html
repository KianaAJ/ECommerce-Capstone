All to Know About Synthwave

The History Of Synthwave

Synthwave is a music genre that emerged in the mid-2000s, characterized by its nostalgic homage to the sounds and aesthetics of 1980s electronic and pop culture. It draws inspiration from the synthesizer-driven soundtracks of iconic movies and video games from that era, as well as the pulsating beats of early electronic dance music. Synthwave artists often use vintage synthesizers and drum machines to create a retro-futuristic sound that evokes a sense of nostalgia for a bygone era. The genre gained popularity through online platforms and social media, with artists like Kavinsky, College, and Miami Nights 1984 contributing to its rise. Synthwave's blend of nostalgia and modern production techniques has since spawned numerous subgenres and a dedicated fan base, making it a prominent and enduring part of contemporary electronic music culture.

Synthwave's Effects on Art Today

Synthwave has had a profound and far-reaching impact on contemporary art, reshaping creative landscapes across various disciplines. This genre's nostalgic homage to the 1980s, characterized by neon-infused aesthetics and retro-futuristic visions, has breathed new life into the art world. The influence of synthwave resonates in vibrant color palettes, bold digital art forms, and a fascination with retro-futurism, all of which have reinvigorated modern artistic expression. It has forged connections between music and visual art, enabling collaborations that seamlessly blend auditory and visual experiences. Additionally, synthwave's striking influence is evident in the resurgence of retro fashion trends, the dynamic world of street art, architectural and interior design choices, and even its infiltration into cinema, television, and broader pop culture. Synthwave has, without a doubt, left an indelible imprint on contemporary art, bridging nostalgia with futurism in ways that continue to inspire and captivate artists and audiences alike.

What Defines Synthwave?

  • Neon Aesthetics: Synthwave art often features a prominent neon color palette, with vibrant pinks, blues, and purples. This choice of colors is a nod to the neon signs and futuristic visuals of the 1980s.
  • 80s Pop Culture References: Synthwave artists frequently incorporate references to iconic 1980s pop culture, including movies like "Blade Runner" and "The Terminator," as well as video games like "Outrun." These references resonate with fans of the era.
  • Retro Futurism: Synthwave art captures the essence of retro-futurism, blending the optimism of how people in the '80s envisioned the future with the technology of the time. It often features elements like futuristic cityscapes, retro cars, and high-tech gadgets.
  • VHS Tape Aesthetics: To evoke the feel of the '80s, synthwave art sometimes incorporates VHS tape glitches, tracking lines, and scan errors. This adds an authentic retro touch to the visuals.
